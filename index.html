Before you begin

This sprint is designed for people navigating real constraints: financial, family, health, uncertainty. You do not need to be ready to make a big decision. You just need 10 minutes and a willingness to notice what is already true.

Missed a day? That is fine. Pick up where you left off, or skip to today. There is no right way to do this. Just forward.

What Is Actually in My Control?

Clarity

🧠 Brain Science

When things feel uncertain, your brain's threat system (the amygdala) becomes more active. It scans for risk and tries to protect you, often by focusing on what could go wrong. This can make everything feel bigger and more overwhelming than it actually is.

✍️ Your Exercise

Use the space below to list everything on your mind right now under three headings:

  • In my control
  • Not in my control
  • In my control, but risky right now

That third category matters. Some things are technically controllable but feel off-limits given your finances, family, or situation. Naming them is itself relieving.

📝 Your Notes
💡 Why This Works

This shifts activity from your threat system to your prefrontal cortex, the part responsible for decision-making and clarity. Your brain cannot problem-solve when it thinks you are in danger.

What Are You Telling Yourself?

Awareness

🧠 Brain Science

Your brain fills in gaps with predictions. When you do not have information, it often defaults to worst-case scenarios to keep you safe. A lot of career stress comes from anticipation and uncertainty, not just workload. The story you tell yourself while waiting is often more exhausting than the situation itself.

✍️ Your Exercise

Think of something that has been uncertain or unresolved for you lately: a decision pending, a situation unclear, a conversation not yet had.

Write it down, then ask: what am I telling myself about this? Is that story helping me, or just filling the silence?

Then label each thought you notice:

  • Fact: I know this to be true
  • Interpretation: this is my read on things
  • Fear-based prediction: this is what I am afraid might happen
📝 Your Notes
💡 Why This Works

You are separating signal from story. Most career stress lives in the story layer, not the facts. Labelling thoughts reduces their emotional charge without requiring you to fix the situation.

Tiny Move

Action

🧠 Brain Science

Motivation does not come first. Action creates motivation. Taking even a small action releases dopamine, which reinforces forward movement and reduces the feeling of being stuck.

✍️ Your Exercise

Choose one option based on where you are today:

⏱ 5 minutesSend one message or write one thought down.
⏱ 15 minutesHave one informal conversation or read one thing that interests you.
🪫 Completely drainedDo nothing external. Just write one sentence about what is in your way right now.

That last option matters. If something is blocking small steps, naming it is the step.

📝 Your Notes
💡 Why This Works

You are training your brain to associate action with progress, not fear. The drained option ensures you stay in the sprint even on hard days, and what you write becomes useful data for Day 4.

What Is Actually in the Way?

Constraint Mapping

🧠 Brain Science

When your nervous system is dysregulated (through stress, anxiety, or burnout), your brain prioritises survival over thinking clearly. People often do not know what a small step looks like, not because they lack ideas, but because something unnamed is blocking them. Surfacing that block is the real work.

✍️ Your Exercise

Ask yourself honestly: what is making it hard to move forward right now? Check anything that resonates:

  • Low energy or burnout
  • Fear of being seen trying and failing
  • Unclear what is realistic given my finances or situation
  • Waiting for the big thing to change before anything else can
  • Do not know what a small step even looks like here
  • Something else
📝 Your Notes
💡 Why This Works

Naming the real block reduces its power. You are not trying to solve it today, just make it visible. This helps your brain stop treating the obstacle as a threat and start treating it as information.

Expanding Possibilities

Possibility

🧠 Brain Science

When stressed, your brain narrows its perception of options (called cognitive constriction). It tries to simplify the world to reduce risk, which means possibilities you would normally see become invisible.

✍️ Your Exercise

List 10 possible directions split across two columns. No filter. No judgment. Unrealistic ones are welcome. They often point to what you actually want.

Within my current life

If things were different

💡 Why This Works

You are widening your brain's perception of possibility, which reduces the feeling of being trapped. The two-column approach often surprises people: there are usually more options within their current life than they thought.

The Temporary Reframe

Perspective

🧠 Brain Science

Your brain treats permanent situations as higher risk. When something feels endless, threat increases, and so does the pressure to solve it completely before moving.

✍️ Your Exercise

You may not be able to leave your situation right now, and that is real. This prompt is not asking you to pretend otherwise.

Within the situation I am already in: what is one thing I could do differently that would make today feel even slightly more like mine?

The word yours is doing the work here. You are not changing the situation. You are reclaiming a small piece of agency inside it. Then act on one thing from that answer today.

📝 Your Notes
💡 Why This Works

This reduces perceived threat and increases psychological flexibility, without requiring you to pretend your constraints do not exist. Small acts of self-determination, even inside a constrained situation, are genuinely restorative.

What Changed?

Reflection

🧠 Brain Science

Your brain is constantly updating based on experience. Reflection helps consolidate new patterns. Without it, insights fade. Ending with a forward anchor, not just looking back, matters: people who feel like their career is happening to them need to leave with something they are choosing, not just something they noticed.

✍️ Your Exercise

Reflect on the past 7 days:

  • Where did I feel more in control this week?
  • What small actions made a difference?
  • What feels even slightly different from Day 1?
  • What is the smallest thing I want to keep doing after this week?
📝 Your Notes
💡 Why This Works

Reflection alone closes the loop. A forward anchor keeps momentum going. Ending with a choice, not just an insight, is how the sprint returns agency to you. That is the whole point.
